Cutting through the PQC noise — on my laptop

Real handshakes. Real numbers. Two migration scenarios tested on an HP Envy i7.

Kishore Chavali·Data Security Architect·Bangalore, India·April 2025

Why I built this lab

Over the past few months, PQC has been difficult to ignore — and I mean that in the best possible way. I have worked through the NIST finalised standards, NCSC and CISA migration guidance, crypto agility frameworks, position papers, vendor whitepapers, and a growing wave of cryptographic posture management and discovery tooling. The breadth of material reflects how seriously the industry is taking this shift. It also reflects how much noise has gathered around a genuinely important problem.

What struck me was the sheer range of signals coming in at once: genuine technical urgency sitting right next to significant marketing hype, real prioritisation problems buried under an avalanche of "harvest now, decrypt later" anxiety, and vendors pitching discovery tools before most organisations even have a clear view of what they are trying to discover.

Somewhere in the middle of all that, a few practical questions kept surfacing:

What actually needs to happen first?

What is the minimum viable migration step that builds momentum without requiring a full PKI overhaul?

And what do the NIST algorithms actually cost at the handshake level — on real hardware, not in a vendor benchmark?

Discovery and inventory are clearly foundational — you cannot migrate what you have not mapped. But I wanted to go a step further and understand the migration patterns themselves: what is genuinely easy to do today, what requires planning, and where the real operational cost lies.

So I set up a small lab on my personal HP Envy i7, built the Open Quantum Safe stack from source, generated both classical and post-quantum certificate chains, and ran two distinct TLS migration scenarios against each other. What follows are the actual results — commands, screenshots, handshake output, and benchmark numbers — with no vendor involvement and no synthetic environment.

This is a practitioner's PoC, not a production deployment guide. The goal is to build an informed intuition for the migration decision — and to give other architects something concrete to point at when the conversation turns to "but what does PQC actually cost?"

The lab stack

Everything runs on Ubuntu 24.04 (WSL2 on Windows 11) on the HP Envy i7 — no cloud, no dedicated server. The core components are the Open Quantum Safe project's liboqs 0.10.1 and oqs-provider 0.6.1, which bridges liboqs into OpenSSL's provider architecture. Scenario B runs inside the openquantumsafe/oqs-ossl3 Docker image to work around an OpenSSL 3.0.x PEM encoder limitation for hybrid KEM key types.

BASH · Build stack
# Build liboqs — core PQ algorithm library
git clone --depth 1 --branch 0.10.1 https://github.com/open-quantum-safe/liboqs.git
cmake -GNinja -B build -DOQS_USE_OPENSSL=ON -DBUILD_SHARED_LIBS=ON -DOPENSSL_ROOT_DIR=/usr
ninja -C build && ninja -C build install

# Build oqs-provider — bridges liboqs into OpenSSL 3.x provider API
git clone --depth 1 --branch 0.6.1 https://github.com/open-quantum-safe/oqs-provider.git
cmake -GNinja -B build -Dliboqs_DIR=/usr/local/lib/cmake/liboqs
ninja -C build
sudo cp build/lib/oqsprovider.so /usr/lib/x86_64-linux-gnu/ossl-modules/

The two scenarios

I tested two distinct migration paths that reflect the actual decision enterprises face today.

Scenario A — KEM only
Replace key exchange with X25519+MLKEM768 hybrid (FIPS 203). Classical ECDSA P-256 certificate chain stays unchanged. Zero PKI impact.

Scenario A is the headline result: Hybrid KEM (X25519+MLKEM768) is not just acceptable — on loopback it outperforms classical X25519 due to lower latency variance. The migration cost is essentially zero. This is why Chrome, Cloudflare, and AWS are already shipping it in production.

Key takeaways for security architects

  • 01Start Scenario A now. Hybrid KEM (X25519+MLKEM768) is already in Chrome, Cloudflare, and AWS. Near-zero performance cost. No PKI changes. The IETF hybrid design draft (0x11eb) is the emerging standard. There is no good reason to wait.
  • 02Scenario B needs a PKI programme, not a config change. ML-DSA-65 cert sizes will cascade through your CA hierarchy, OCSP infrastructure, CRL distribution, WAF buffer configs, and any system that stores or transmits certs. Size up the blast radius before you migrate.
  • 03"Harvest now, decrypt later" targets the KEM — not the signature. The quantum threat to confidentiality is active now. The threat to authentication has a longer horizon. Fix the KEM first — that is Scenario A.
  • 04You cannot migrate what you have not inventoried. Build a Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM) before you migrate. Know every TLS endpoint, every cipher suite, every cert algorithm, every HSM key type in your estate. Discovery is the hard part — migration is mechanical once you have the map.
